01 — The Two Acquisition Paths

After 2015, there are
only two ways to get a mine.

Private companies and government PSUs operate in completely separate systems — with radically different cost outcomes. Understanding these two paths is the foundation of understanding the entire Indian steel industry's competitive landscape.

04 — How The Payments Actually Work

Breaking down
each formula step by step.

The complexity of the two-path system makes it easy to lose the plot. Here is exactly how the government's cut is calculated — for each type of player. Select a route below.

  • 1

    Extract ore and report quantity

    Every month, the company measures and reports the quantity of iron ore extracted in metric tonnes to the state government. This forms the base of every payment calculation.

  • 2

    Look up the official benchmark price

    The Indian Bureau of Mines (IBM) publishes a monthly official market price for iron ore. This benchmark — not the company's actual sale price — is used for all royalty and premium calculations.

  • 3

    Apply the auction premium — the dominant cost

    The premium % the company bid during the e-auction is multiplied against quantity × benchmark price. At 141.25%, this single line item dwarfs all other payments combined.

    Tata Steel bid: 141.25%
  • 4

    Add the statutory base royalty (15%)

    All miners — private and PSU alike — pay a base royalty set by the central government. For iron ore it is currently 15% of the IBM benchmark value. This is the floor everyone pays.

  • 5

    Add DMF, NMET, and state cesses

    District Mineral Foundation (30% of royalty), National Mineral Exploration Trust (2% of royalty), and any state-specific levies stack on top. Every rupee of base royalty multiplies these contributions too.

Monthly Payment =
Qty × IBM Price × (141.25% premium + 15% royalty + ~3.8% levies)
≈ ~156–160% of ore's market value paid to government per tonne

    Central or State government designates a mineral block for a PSU without any competitive process. No e-auction is held, no bids are placed, and no auction premium is ever established for this mine.

  • 2

    Pay the same 15% statutory base royalty

    PSUs are not exempt from the base royalty. The same 15% of IBM benchmark value applies to every tonne mined — this is identical to what private companies pay on this component.

  • 3

    Pay the mandatory Additional Royalty — the levelling mechanism

    Because PSUs bypassed the auction, the law mandates an Additional Royalty equal to 150% of the base royalty. This is the government's mechanism to ensure PSUs cannot enjoy a completely free ride while private firms bleed in auctions.

    Additional Royalty = 150% × Base = 22.5% of ore value
  • 4

    Add DMF, NMET, and state cesses

    Same levy structure as private players — DMF at 30% of royalty, NMET at 2%, and applicable state cesses. These calculate off the base royalty, not the Additional Royalty.

05 — How We Got Here

The events that
shaped the hierarchy.

The cost structure of Indian steel today is not an accident. It is the cumulative result of specific decisions made at specific moments in time.

Pre-2015

The Old Regime — First Come, First Served

Mining leases in India were granted at the government's discretion — often without competitive processes. Companies could secure long-term leases through administrative allocations, frequently at minimal cost. There was no standardized auction mechanism.

No market mechanism
2007

LMEL Secures the Surjagarh Iron Ore Block

Lloyds Metals & Energy Ltd receives a mining lease for the Surjagarh deposit in Gadchiroli, Maharashtra — one of the largest high-grade iron ore blocks in central India. Under the rules of the time, no auction premium exists. They pay only the base statutory royalty.

The golden ticket is issued
2015

MMDR Amendment — The Game Changes

The Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Amendment Act of 2015 mandates that all new mineral leases for specified minerals (including iron ore) must be granted through competitive electronic auctions. The Additional Royalty framework for PSUs is simultaneously introduced. Pre-existing leases are grandfathered in.

New era begins
2015–Present

The Auction Bloodbath — Premiums Soar

As auctions become the only legal route for new private mines, desperate steel companies begin bidding extraordinary premiums. Tata Steel bids 141.25% in one notable auction. JSW and others follow. The cost gap between legacy miners and new entrants becomes staggering.

Auction premiums exceed 100%
Present — 2057

LMEL Operates Under Frozen Economics

Surjagarh is now in active mining and steel production. LMEL's lease terms — established in 2007 — remain unchanged. The company pays approximately 20% of ore value to the government while competitors pay 40%–150%+. This advantage is not contingent on management decisions, commodity cycles, or policy changes. It is a legal fact.

Moat locked until 2057
